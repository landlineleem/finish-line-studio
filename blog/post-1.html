5 Things to Prepare Before Your Install Day

By Sarah Derrick  ·  Finish Line Studio, Austin

← Back to Journal

The best install days don't happen by accident. Behind every smooth reveal is a set of preparations made the night before — or even the week before. After years on the install floor, I've seen the same avoidable scrambles repeat themselves on job after job. Here's the five-point checklist I walk through before every project.

1. Confirm your delivery schedule in writing

Nothing derails an install day faster than a delivery window that turns into a four-hour wait. The evening before, confirm every incoming delivery by email or text — not just the main freight, but the specialty pieces, the flooring, the accessories. Get a window, get a name, and make sure whoever is receiving has the right contact number. If something is backordered, it's better to know at 8pm than at 8am.

2. Walk the site (or have someone else do it)

If you can get access to the space the day before installation, do it. Take photos. Note any last-minute construction debris that wasn't cleared, measure the awkward hallway the rug has to travel through, confirm the drapery rods are actually installed. Five minutes of walkthrough time prevents hours of problem-solving under pressure.

3. Pack a contingency kit

There's a small kit that travels with me to every install: extra picture hooks, felt pads in multiple sizes, a level, blue painter's tape, a small steamer, stitch witchery, and a lint roller. It weighs almost nothing and has saved at least a dozen installs. Ask your support team what's in their kit — or better yet, ask Finish Line Studio to bring theirs.

4. Set client expectations for timeline

A common source of install day stress is a client who expects to walk through their completed home at noon. Be honest about the timeline: multi-room installs often take 8–10 hours, and the last 10% of the work — the styling, the florals, the final sweep — takes longer than people expect. Send a brief update the night before confirming the reveal window. Under-promise, over-deliver.

5. Eat a real breakfast

This sounds trivial until you've been on your feet for six hours and the only food available is a protein bar from a client's kitchen. Install days are physically demanding — lifting rugs, climbing ladders, steaming drapery. Eat well before you arrive. Bring snacks. If your team is working with you, bring enough to share. A well-fed crew is a focused crew.

The goal of all this preparation isn't to eliminate surprise — surprises happen on every install day. The goal is to make sure that when the unexpected arrives, you're not also dealing with an avoidable problem you could have caught the night before.
